## Epilepsy and the brain

Epilepsy is a neurological condition defined by recurrent seizures arising from abnormal electrical activity in the brain. It affects roughly one in every hundred people and can occur at any age. The type, frequency, and location of seizures vary widely, as do their effects on daily life.

Beyond seizures themselves, many people with epilepsy experience cognitive and emotional challenges including difficulties with memory, attention, processing speed, and mood. These effects can result from the underlying cause of the epilepsy, the seizures themselves, or the side effects of anti-seizure medications.

## Role of neuropsychological assessment

Neuropsychological assessment is a standard component of epilepsy care. It serves several important functions:

- **Baseline evaluation:** Establishing a cognitive baseline at diagnosis or before treatment changes allows future assessments to track progression or improvement.
- **Pre-surgical planning:** For people being considered for epilepsy surgery, neuropsychological assessment helps localise cognitive functions and estimate the risk of post-surgical deficits.
- **Post-surgical follow-up:** Assessment after surgery documents changes in cognitive function and guides rehabilitation.
- **Monitoring:** Regular assessment tracks cognitive effects of ongoing seizure activity or medication adjustments.

Dr. Benjamin has specialised expertise in epilepsy neuropsychology, including pre-surgical evaluation and brain mapping, and is a member of the neuropsychology team at Vancouver General Hospital's epilepsy clinic.

## What to expect

Assessment involves a clinical interview and a comprehensive battery of cognitive tests covering memory, language, attention, processing speed, and executive function. Testing typically takes four to six hours and may be conducted across two sessions. Results are communicated in a written report with recommendations for the referring team and the patient.
