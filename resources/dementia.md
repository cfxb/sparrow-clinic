## What is dementia?

Dementia is an umbrella term for a group of conditions characterised by a progressive decline in cognitive function severe enough to interfere with daily life. The most common form is Alzheimer's disease, but dementia can also result from vascular disease, Lewy body pathology, frontotemporal degeneration, and other causes.

Early symptoms typically include memory difficulties — particularly for recent events — alongside changes in language, reasoning, or spatial orientation. As dementia progresses, it affects a wider range of cognitive abilities and independence.

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) is a transitional stage between normal aging and dementia. People with MCI have cognitive changes beyond what is expected for their age, but remain largely independent. Not everyone with MCI develops dementia, but it represents an important period for monitoring and intervention.

## Why neuropsychological assessment matters

Neuropsychological assessment is central to the diagnosis and management of dementia and MCI. It provides:

- **Early and differential diagnosis:** Standardised testing can detect subtle cognitive changes before they are apparent to family members or on brief clinical screening. It also helps distinguish between different causes of dementia, which have different implications for treatment and prognosis.
- **Baseline documentation:** Establishing a cognitive baseline early in the course of symptoms allows future assessments to track the rate and pattern of change.
- **Distinguishing normal ageing:** Not every memory complaint reflects a pathological process. Neuropsychological assessment provides an objective comparison against age-appropriate norms.
- **Informing care planning:** Understanding the specific pattern of cognitive strengths and weaknesses helps individuals, families, and care teams make informed decisions about daily support, safety, and planning for the future.

## What the assessment involves

A dementia-focused neuropsychological assessment includes a clinical interview with the patient and, where appropriate, a family member or carer. Cognitive testing covers memory, language, attention, visuospatial abilities, and executive function. Testing typically takes two to four hours.

The written report provides a detailed interpretation of findings, a diagnostic impression where possible, and recommendations for the patient, family, and referring physician.
